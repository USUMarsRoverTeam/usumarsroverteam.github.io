## Science Module

To improve the science module in future iterations, the team should assign dedicated personnel to it at project kickoff. A cross-disciplinary subgroup that includes at least one student with some level of biology or chemistry background, not solely mechanical engineering, will allow for a focus on the assay delivery and mixing system. Establishing science-specific milestones early in the Gantt chart would ensure progress on life-detection tasks proceeds in parallel with mechanical fabrication, reducing the risk of failing URC acceptance due to an under-developed science module.

Sensor integration has not yet been completed and will need to have the coding worked out and integrated into our ROS2 system. This entails taking readouts from each sensor, saving them as a txt or csv file, then beaming it out to the ground station to perform data analysis during the competition. There may also be issues with the sensors that have been picked because they have not yet gone through rigorous testing to prove whether or not they are best for this program.

Next, plan a realistic consumables budget. Reagents such as hydrogen peroxide, luciferase, ATP standards, and certified control soils are expendable and costly, especially when multiple calibration rounds are required. Future teams should create a "science expendables" line item in their budget plan. Getting in contact with the university's life-sciences department or local biotech sponsors early can offset these costs and secure a reliable supply of test kits and disposables well before integration.

Finally, consider building the connectors for the science module chassis. The current 0.5-inch core routinely collects 15 g of soil, far exceeding the URC requirement yet adding mass, torque demand, and volumetric footprint. Redesigning the coring bit to operate with a smaller rotating drill motor to reduce bending moments on the linear actuator.
